The ways to Santiago

Camino Inglés

The short one. A whole Camino feeling in a single week.

Distance
116 km
Time
5 to 6 days
Stages
5 to 6 stages
Difficulty
Moderate

Starts in Ferrol, Spain

The Inglés is the route that medieval pilgrims from England and Ireland walked after they had sailed to the Galician coast. From Ferrol it clears the 100 km required for the Compostela, and that makes it the most complete short Camino there is.

Short, though, is far from flat. The days roll constantly through eucalyptus forest and farmland, and the arrival in Santiago feels exactly as earned.

History and character

The Camino Inglés, the English Route, is also called the Old Royal Route. It is especially known among pilgrims from Britain and Ireland, who once sailed to the Galician port towns and walked the last stretch to Santiago on foot. It is a historic trade route with culture and nature all the way.

It typically starts in one of the two port towns, Ferrol or A Coruña, and it is one of the shortest of the well-known Caminos. From Ferrol it is around 120 kilometres, and with a fine start by the water you get the sea before you head into the green Galician landscape.

The route is becoming more and more popular, but in 2019 it was still only around 4.5 percent of everyone who reached Santiago who had walked it. That makes it a quiet and deeply rewarding alternative to the busiest routes.

Highlights along the way

1

Ferrol

A historic naval town by a deep natural harbour. The starting point where the seafaring pilgrims disembarked and began to walk.

2

Pontedeume

A charming town with a medieval bridge and beautiful views over the Eume river. One of the fine early stops on the route.

3

Betanzos

Known for its historic old town with steep cobbled streets and Gothic churches. A good place to rest after a solid stage.

4

Hospital de Bruma

An important stop after one of the more demanding stages with some climbs. The sense of achievement on arrival is great.

5

The Galician forest

Rolling stages through eucalyptus and pine with the damp, green silence that Galicia is known for.

6

The arrival in Santiago

The last kilometres via Sigüeiro into the cathedral feel just as big as on the long routes. A full Camino in a week.

Terrain and difficulty

The Inglés is short, but not at all flat. It offers both flat sections and moderate climbs through eucalyptus forest and farmland, and a couple of the middle stages, including the stretch in towards Hospital de Bruma, take good fitness. The surface changes between forest paths, country roads and cobbles through the towns. If you choose to start in A Coruña rather than Ferrol, the route is only around 75 kilometres. That is too short to give you the Compostela, which requires at least 100 kilometres, so if you want the pilgrim certificate, you have to start in Ferrol. Galicia's weather can bring rain and damp all year round, so bring proper rain gear.

Who the route suits

The Inglés is an excellent choice if you only have a week but still want to walk a whole Camino with a real pilgrim certificate at the end. The route can be completed in 5 to 7 days, and it is ideal for you if you do not have time to commit to the long routes. It is also a nice first taste and a good way to experience Galicia's culture and landscape without the big crowds.

Getting to the start

Ferrol is easily reached from A Coruña, which has an airport with connections to several European cities, and trains and buses run onward along the coast. From Denmark the way typically goes via Madrid or another Spanish airport and onward to Galicia. Collect your pilgrim passport in Ferrol and start at the harbour, where the seafaring pilgrims once came ashore.

The route on the map

When to go

April to October. It is also a fine winter route, Galicia stays mild, but bring proper rain gear all year round.

Frequently asked questions about the Camino Inglés

How long is the Camino Inglés, and how long does it take?
From Ferrol it is around 116 kilometres and takes 5 to 6 days. It can be completed in 5 to 7 days.
Where does it start and end?
It starts in the port town of Ferrol and ends in Santiago. It is the route medieval pilgrims from England and Ireland walked after they had sailed to the Galician coast.
Can I earn the pilgrim certificate on the Camino Inglés?
Yes, if you start in Ferrol, which is around 116 kilometres. If you start in A Coruña, it is only around 75 kilometres, which is too short for the Compostela, which requires at least 100 kilometres.
Is the Camino Inglés hard?
It is short, but not flat. There are moderate climbs through eucalyptus forest and farmland, and a couple of the middle stages in towards Hospital de Bruma take good fitness.
When is the best time of year?
April to October. It also works as a winter route, since Galicia stays mild, but bring proper rain gear all year round.
Roughly how much does it cost?
Count on around 30 to 40 euros a day for hostels and pilgrim menus.
