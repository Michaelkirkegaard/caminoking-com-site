Can You Cycle the Camino? A Guide to Your Pilgrimage on Two Wheels

Yes, you can cycle the Camino de Santiago. Here is what to expect from the terrain, the daily distances and how to prepare your bike and body.

Every year thousands of pilgrims set out for Santiago de Compostela on the Camino. Most of them walk, but there is a growing group of people who experience the pilgrimage from the saddle instead. So can you actually cycle the Camino, and is it a rewarding way to do it?

The answer is yes. I have cycled stretches of several Camino routes myself, and while I have not yet ridden the full Camino Francés, I can tell you it is a completely different experience from walking. Neither is better than the other. It comes down to taste, time and what you want out of the journey.

One practical thing to understand from the start: as a cyclist you share much of the path with walkers, but there is also a dedicated cycling route. Some sections of the walking trail are simply not rideable, so the bike route detours around them.

Good Reasons to Cycle the Camino

The last time I walked the Camino Francés, in 2022, a familiar figure came rolling past on a bike: Ronaldo Luís Nazário de Lima, the legendary Brazilian striker. If one of the greatest forwards in football history feels like cycling the Camino, that should be reason enough for the rest of us. But here are a few more.

Speed and Distance

Cycling lets you cover far more ground per day than walking. A typical cyclist can expect to ride somewhere between 50 and 70 kilometres a day, depending on the terrain. That makes the bike especially attractive if your holiday is short. A route that takes a walker a month can fit inside a normal vacation on two wheels.

A Different Perspective

On a bike the landscape moves. The views change constantly, there is a breeze on your face, and the whole journey has a rhythm and momentum that walking simply does not have. Some people find that dynamic feeling addictive. Others miss the slowness of walking. If you have cycled a Camino yourself, I would genuinely love to hear how it felt, and whether the walkers treated you kindly when you rang your bell behind them.

The Challenges of Cycling the Camino

It is not all tailwind and open road. Two things deserve honest mention before you commit.

Terrain

The Camino covers hugely varied ground, from flat paved roads to rough mountain paths. Certain stretches, especially on the Camino Francés, are technically demanding on a bike. Loose stone, steep gradients and narrow tracks will test both your equipment and your nerve. This is exactly why the dedicated cycling variants exist, and why it pays to study your route before each stage.

Logistics

Not every albergue along the way has facilities for bikes. Secure storage overnight is not a given, so you need to plan your route and your accommodation with the bike in mind. A quick check before booking saves you from standing outside a full hostel at dusk with nowhere safe to leave your most important piece of equipment.

How to Prepare for Cycling the Camino

Choose the Right Bike

A sturdy touring bike or a mountain bike is ideal for the Camino’s mixed terrain. Whatever you ride, make sure it is well maintained before you leave: good tyres, reliable brakes and gears that shift cleanly. A bike problem in a small Spanish village on a Sunday is a problem you want to avoid.

Pack Smart

On top of the usual travel essentials, bring a bike repair kit, a spare inner tube and the tools for basic maintenance. Punctures do not care about your schedule. Also carry water and snacks, because some stretches run long between shops and cafés, and a hungry cyclist is a slow cyclist.

If you want a full rundown of what to bring on the Camino in general, have a look through our guides.

Train Before You Go

Prepare your body for long days in the saddle. Start with shorter rides at home and build gradually toward longer distances. You are training two things at once: your endurance and your comfort on the bike. Saddle soreness is real, and the only cure is time in the saddle before the trip, so your body has adapted by the time you reach the Pyrenees.

Planning Your Route

Research the different Camino routes to find the most bike-friendly one for you. The Camino Francés is the most popular choice among cyclists, with the best infrastructure and the most company along the way. The Camino del Norte is another fantastic option if you prefer coastal views and less traffic, though it is hillier and tougher on the legs.

Whichever you choose, the destination is the same: the cathedral square in Santiago de Compostela, where cyclists and walkers roll and limp in side by side, all of them pilgrims.

Buen Camino, and happy riding.
