VO2 Max: Boost Your Outdoor Performance with Oxygen Uptake

If you are ready to improve your outdoor experiences, it is time to take a closer look at VO2 max. This number can transform your bike rides, cross-country adventures, hikes and runs.

What is VO2 max?

VO2 max, also known as maximal oxygen uptake, is a measure of the maximum amount of oxygen your body can take in and use during intense physical activity. It is often expressed in millilitres of oxygen per kilogram of body weight per minute (ml/kg/min) and serves as an indicator of your cardiorespiratory fitness.

What is a good VO2 max?

A “good” VO2 max varies depending on factors such as age, sex and training level. Generally, a VO2 max above the average level for your age group is considered good. See the tables below for a detailed breakdown of normal values for women and men.

Is VO2 max the same as fitness score?

Although the terms are often used interchangeably, VO2 max and fitness score are not quite the same. Fitness score generally refers to the body’s ability to transport and use oxygen during aerobic activities. While VO2 max measures maximum oxygen consumption, fitness score relates to how efficiently the body uses oxygen at moderate to high intensity.

How do you calculate VO2 max?

VO2 max can be measured accurately through specialised tests such as an incremental treadmill test or a bike test, where the participant exercises at increasing intensity while oxygen and carbon dioxide levels are measured. For a rougher estimate you can use approximate formulas, but these do not give results as precise as professional tests.

How to boost your VO2 max: training and tips

To improve your VO2 max, and therefore your performance, training is the key. Here are some tips:

  1. Interval training: Add short periods of intense exercise followed by rest or low intensity. This challenges the cardiovascular system and improves VO2 max.
  2. Long, moderate training: Add longer training sessions at moderate intensity to improve your body’s ability to work at a sustained oxygen uptake.
  3. Strength training: Build muscle strength to reduce the amount of oxygen required at any given activity. Simple tools like kettlebells and dumbbells go a long way.
  4. Altitude training: Training at altitude with lower oxygen pressure can stimulate the production of red blood cells and improve VO2 max.

Let the adventure begin

Use the tables and guidelines as a starting point to understand your cardiorespiratory fitness, and set goals for improvement.
