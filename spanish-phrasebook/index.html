How to read the pronunciation. Capital letters mark the stressed syllable. J sounds like a soft h, ll like y, and ñ like ny. In Spain many lisp c and z into a soft th, as in thing, but a plain s is understood everywhere too.

Frequently asked questions about Spanish on the Camino

Do I need to speak Spanish to walk the Camino?
No. A great many people walk the whole way with almost no Spanish. But a few phrases make daily life easier and the encounters warmer. If you can say Buen Camino, gracias and por favor, you are already well on your way.
What is the single most important phrase to know?
Buen Camino. It means good way, and it is the greeting pilgrims and locals use to each other the whole way. After it come gracias, meaning thank you, and por favor, meaning please.
Do people really say gracias as grah-thyas?
In Spain many say grah-thyas with a soft lisping th on c and z, roughly like the th in the English word thing. But grah-syas is understood everywhere, so choose whichever you are most comfortable with. The important thing is that you try.
What are the most common words at the hostel?
An albergue is a hostel, a cama is a bed, and a sello is the stamp you get in your credencial. With the phrase ¿Hay camas libres? you ask whether there are free beds.
How do I order a pilgrim menu?
Say El menú del peregrino, por favor. It is the set three-course meal at a fixed price you find all along the route. If you want a small draught beer with it, that is una caña, por favor. See more about food on the trail.
What do I do if I need help?
Learn a few emergency phrases by heart before you go. Necesito ayuda means I need help, me duele el pie means my foot hurts, and ¿Dónde está la farmacia? asks where the pharmacy is. The Spanish emergency number is 112.
